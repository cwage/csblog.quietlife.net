![UX & Marketing](http://blog.centresource.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/Venn-Diagram-1429282763526680bb-c7de-4fa5-8b51-891cae1f7ddf-300x225.jpg)I often see an unjustified stratification between UX and marketing goals. These work together, hand-in-hand to achieve great success for a product or service. Examples of assimilation are frequently used in partnership with intentional UI decisions. A UI (user interface) is the so-called "skin" of a product that plays into the aesthetic of the marketing materials. Each have their specific purposes but all of which make up the user's overall experience (UX).

Marketing goals are set and through a great UX, the user can achieve said goals. Mechanisms such as clear and persistent calls-to-action (CTAs) contribute toward a common marketing goal of customer engagement. By having an outlet for a user to take a next step (contact us, view products, etc.) there aren't any dead ends. User experiences often fall into the category of unknown but when situations are thoroughly thought through, marketing goals are very easy to achieve. That said, achievement is a poor term, as growth comes iteratively. Goals can be reached but they should then be exceeded, reassessed, and redefined.

One might ask, 'how do i reassess and redefine goals?' To that I say, let the data drive your decision making. Look at the analytics and make an informed decision based on user behavior. A good example of this might be a common scenario where a product owner is thinking about adding a new feature - they make the assumption that by adding feature X their product will appeal to a broader audience and the existing audience will continue to use it. A method of testing this might be the use of a 'phantom button'. This is a button that does nothing but trigger a popover telling the user that this feature is coming soon. What this allows us to do on the analytics end is measure interest in the feature. Maybe feature X returns very little interest and is not worth the investment to build out that feature. That cash can now be used for other, more proven opportunities and marketing goals.

Another great intersection of marketing goals and UX is where solicitation of customer feedback comes in. It is important to listen to your customers, and doing this is a delicate task. You don't want to bug them to a point of annoyance but you also don't want to be ignorant of what their lingering needs might be. I like to think of this interaction as more of an exchange - a situation where both sides have skin in the game. Marketing goals may ask for feedback that requires a semi-significant time commitment, and one way to incentivize this is through providing free content in exchange for their time. We have seen that this 'ask' is suddenly less daunting to users who know what they get in return.

Overall there should not be a stratification between UX and marketing goals, rather, these two should twirl together in a symbiotic relationship of give and take. As always, let data drive your decision making, especially as it pertains to value analysis and ROI.
