Evolution can be defined as iterative innovation. Therefore, without innovation, growth is choked. At Centresource, we believe in embracing change, as change aids our overall growth. We are constantly evolving as a company and the fruits of our growth lies in our finely tuned process. There are two prominent models for effective design - 'Agile' and 'Waterfall'. We have tried both of these models and through careful evaluation have quantifiably merged the two ideologies to create a hybrid we like to call the 'House' method.

## **The House Method**

![The House Method](https://csblog.quietlife.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/blueprints-300x186.jpg)This methodology derives it's name from our house analogy we use to describe it. Think of building a website or product as building a house - it starts with evaluating the environment and identifying foreseeable obstacles. After this survey of the land is complete, the architect can begin to paint their vision for the 'house' (taking into account the environment's strengths and challenges). These two steps can be equated to our strategy and planning sessions. We evaluate the competitive landscape, user challenges, and business strengths. With this, we create a rough plan of action which bleeds into the very early stages of production.

This rough plan outlines our constraints, our opportunities, and overall direction. Imagine these are the ingredients stirred into the cement at the foundation of the house. It sets the bounds and guides the shape through a strong, level foundation - without it, the house would collapse. An imbalance of any single ingredient can lead to a crumbling or cracked foundation so it is important to carefully mix, pour, and shape.

With a sturdy foundation, we can begin framing the house. These 'bare-bones' shape the rooms; furthermore the layouts are informed by the foundation and architect's careful planning beforehand. As issues arise (and they often do in this phase) we can quickly and easily pivot to make changes without having to rip out wiring or tear down drywall. Efficiency is highest at this most fragile state of the house. For us, this parallels to our wireframing stage. We create rough versions of the product that is intentionally void of any aesthetic or skinning. We do this to force a purely functional approach to the design. We ask ourselves things like "What would the user need to do here?" or "What is their logical next step?" When we realize a functional issue we can easily change the layout (or structural elements) to support the solution, whereas if we were to discover a functional error in a later stage, it would be much harder and more time-consuming to course-correct.

The next step is the fun and often romanticized stage of the build. In terms of our house analogy, this is when the house begins to look like a home. The sheetrock goes up, the walls get painted, the fixtures are added, the art is hung, and the furniture is moved in. Often this is done on a room-by-room basis. Similarly, our team uses this stage to finalize the palette, create consistent user interface styles, define typographic relationships, and make sure the aesthetic accomplishes overarching functional goals outlined in the wireframes.

A frequent misconception is that the design process ends here. To that I'd ask, "would ever you ever build a house without testing the water? How about electricity? What about moving the furniture around a little bit?" I don't think so. Testing is the next phase of our build - we make sure that our goals have been reached, that our informed assumptions are confirmed, and most of all, that the user can happily use the end product. We do this through user-testing, focus groups, and A/B tests informed by Google Analytics.

"That's it! We're done with the design, right?" Not exactly, much like a house, things deteriorate over time. Whether it is through natural deterioration or incidentals, a website or product will need maintenance to combat an eventual collapse. Much like our testing phase, we utilize tools such as Google Analytics to inform when things need maintenance. This iterative stage is conceptually infinite and highly collaborative. We help our clients keep their finger on this pulse as well as quickly react to incidental conditions.

As I mentioned previously, design is a highly collaborative process. Back to our house analogy, lifting and hanging a joist can't be done alone, you need a team who is working together to accomplish the same goal. Some workers are the muscle that lift the joist, others have a precise eye and delicate hand to set it, while others cautiously oversee the whole process to make sure no one gets hurt and the task goes as planned. We work collaboratively with our clients to ensure each of these roles are accounted for and our collective goals are accomplished. Very rarely does any one side of this metaphorical table embody all of these skills, which is why it is important to strike a balance so that no one is trying to lift a joist without a scout to position it or a scout without any muscle - a partnership is needed. Collaboration combats weaknesses through offsetting strengths.

Here at Centresource, we love our partners. We thrive on working with our partners to take a pile of lumber and transform it into a home for your digital ventures to live and grow. Furthermore, we enjoy helping you plan and implement that next unforgettable party (learn more about our [digital marketing offerings](<http://centresource.com/services/digital-marketing/>)) and let's not forget about when you outgrow your house - we are there to help you move and build your next home.

We raise our calloused hand, fortified through more than a decade of expertise and experience, and wave saying "Until next time!"
