I found an old copy of a computer game on my shelf the other day. The game, _[The Sims 2](<http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Sims_2>)_ , had not been installed on my new Mac, and I cannot honestly recall the last time I played it. Being bored at the end of a weekend, I went ahead and played it a bit last night.

If you are unfamiliar with the game, it is a rather interesting concept. You are in control of a Sim (or a handful of Sims) as he or she finds a job, has fun, gains knowledge or whatever other pre-defined aspirations they may have. My first Sim (I called him "Tom Smith" because I am not particularly inventive) got a job as a medical intern and lead a happy life for all of three days. That was until his computer fried and he attempted to repair it. It seems that, much as in real life, it is possible for a Sim to die from electrocution.

The next Sim that I moved in to the house collected Tom into an urn, set him on a bookcase, and promptly called the repair man to fix Tom's computer. But this post is not about the dangers of failing to unplug your electronic devices before haphazardly trying to fix them. It is about personas.

### Stakeholders are not personas

There is a difference between picking one of your primary stakeholders and a persona. Stakeholders represent groups of people (such as prospective customers and existing customers). A persona requires a bit more of an imagination. Let's start with the persona of somebody who might have bought _The Sims 2_.

> Meet (our new urn resident) Tom. Tom is (was?) a 22 year-old man who has just graduated from college with the world ahead of him. Tom aspires to have a family, but right now his social circle is non-existent. He just moved to town to a rather inexpensive house on the outside of Pleasantview. Tom uses his computer to find jobs, but also to play games. Tom has an income of about $32,000 (in Sim-dollars called Simoleans) that he is using to update the one bedroom home. He often calls folks to invite them over to watch TV and play video games. The video game console was an important purchase and set him back a pretty good bit, but it was totally worth it. He gains a lot of happiness from his video game console. He met a few other Sims in the neighborhood by hosting game nights. Tom is not very handy with computer repair.

Now, this one was easy to create. But think about how we can market a computer game to Tom knowing a bit more about him. If I was marketing a single-player game like _The Sims_ to him, it would be tough -- he wants to make friends, not waste time on the computer. If I want to reach people like Tom, I have to make a slight change to my product (two-player?) or I have to convince him that it is a game he should share with his friends. What about a discount code that existing customers can share with others so they can get in on the action? What can be shared from the game with others (houses, furniture, etc.)? Being familiar with these personas is the best way to come up with new ideas to market to them effectively.

Your brand needs multiple personas representing the kinds of consumers that use your product or that you want to use your product. Creating them can be a lot like playing _The Sims_ in that you are most successful when catering what you do to their individual aspirations. If your product or services meets their needs, it is a home-run. Otherwise, they end up going elsewhere to find what they need.

Also, a tip from me to you: Please unplug your computer before working on it. That was a rather sad scene.
