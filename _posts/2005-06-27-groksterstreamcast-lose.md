Well, it's official: Grokster and Streamcast [lost the SCOTUS case](<https://web.archive.org/web/20050629015832/http://www.scotusblog.com/movabletype/archives/2005/06/grokster_stream.html>):

> The Supreme Court ruled unanimously that developers of software violate federal copyright law when they provide computer users with the means to share music and movie files downloaded from the Internet, at least when the software companies take "affirmative steps to foster infringement." 

It seems the lesson here, if anything, is that software developers designing and promoting peer-to-peer file transfer software/networks would do well to not spell out what it can actually be used for. I.e., can you make a program designed to "facilitate transactions of hash-identified chunks of data over a redundant network" and just leave it at that?

I also wonder what taking "affirmative steps to foster infringement" means. If that means you can't plaster your site with specific instructions on how to trade copyrighted material, that's one thing, but if it means you have to bow to every whim of the RIAA and MPAA in implementing restrictions and checks in your software to prevent its abuse, that's quite another.
