Nicholas Holland receives over 200 emails a day. 

According to Technology Market Research Firm Radicati, that is not a shocking number. Their recent study indicated that in 2010, the average person sent and received 110 emails a day.

What's more, according to research by [Harris Interactive](<http://abcnews.go.com/Technology/tech-stress-emails-handle-day/story?id=11201183>), the average person has a stress threshold of 50 emails a day-with anything above that being categorized as unmanageable.

These numbers-as well as their understandable inflation for C-level executives and Senior Managers-make it easy to see why many are beginning to reconsider the way they handle email communication.

For Nicholas Holland-CEO of Interactive Marketing Agency, centresource- a revolutionary shift has been made in the way he handles his inbox.

That is, he no longer opens it.

According to Holland, email "isn't working-it's processing," -a concept he credits to David Allen (inventor of [Getting Things Done](<http://www.davidco.com/>).) For him, emailing is an ongoing task that he says consumes his days and keeps him from reaching optimum levels if productivity.

"For someone like me," says Nicholas, "I get 200+ emails a day so all I do is spend time processing....I realized that a few of my 'bad habits' stemmed from processing all day vs. working."

He goes on to explain that most of his productivity was occurring during the "quiet time" he found after business hours, after his family went to bed; when email was not a concern and his time could be spent focused on actual work.

"I've been amazed at how much email has a negative impact on my work life for over 2 years," says Nicholas, "In fact, I created the "Super Nick" email system just to streamline the processing of huge volumes of email." What's more, Holland asserts, checking email is a "habitual, almost Pavlovian, experience" causing him to check his inbox constantly, often at the sacrifice of movies, dinners, and relational interactions.

So for the last month, as part of an experiment he has entitled "Email Hiatus," Holland no longer sends or receives business emails in favor of other forms of communication. A shift which he insists allows him to spend more time working and less time distracted by the constant, high volume influx of email communication.

With only a few days left in his experiment, Nicholas suggests that what started as research may in fact turn into a lifestyle change. "It originally started as an experiment, but WOW - I don't want to go back. To get to work and avoid email is not only a huge relief, it also means a lot less work at night. Moreover, I'm so happy I don't compulsively check my phone."

The rules of the "Email Hiatus" are simple. Starting 5 days before the official 'launch', any email sent to his professional email address was met with an auto-response explaining the details of the experiment: 1. Any email not marked "IMPORTANT" will be automatically deleted (All emails with 'IMPORTANT' in the subject line are forwarded to his personal email for review.) 2. Instead of email, the options for communication are phone or text message, meeting scheduler app Tungler, Instant Messenger and finally, Twitter.

Additionally, every 2-3 days, Holland reviews his inbox to make sure that nothing critical has slipped through the cracks-making sure to respond to any emails through one of the suggested alternate forms of communication; the ultimate goal being to "stop being reactive and become more proactive."

The response to this experiment, says Holland, has been mixed. With everything from curiosity to animosity to blatant ignoring of the whole thing, his "Email Hiatus" has not gone unnoticed. Most notable, according to Holland, was his father's initial quip that he may as well "start driving a horse and cart to work."

All in all, according to Nicholas, without email he has been more productive, less stressed and more organized. But this email-less month has not come without its set backs. Aside from the freedom of compulsively checking his phone and inbox, he never found another way to sufficiently share electronic files and he is still undecided on how to handle social media alerts that come via email.

"I can't tell if there is some happy medium," Holland concludes. "I really like the concepts of limiting email as a factor of your work day, so perhaps I'll continue with an auto-responder that says I'll only check email at XYZ periods and only if it is directed directly to me."

When all is said and done, he is quick to defend email as a "fantastic, unique, highly versatile form of communication," that, he asserts, is simply suffering the same fate of it's 1980s predecessor: 'meetings'- through abuse, overuse and lack of discernment.

"If people were more discriminating, I wouldn't be as sensitive email - but since it is a one-way communication medium, I definitely believe I'll remain hyper aware of how much I let it control my life."
