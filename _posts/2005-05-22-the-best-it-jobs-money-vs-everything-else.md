The past 2 months continue to bring up a question I've had for a long period. What makes the perfect job? Since CentreSource is a technology firm, the more defined question is "What makes a perfect Technology Job?"

During my stint in corporate America, I often wondered what made me (and others happy). I couldn't fathom doing the same thing, over and over and over and over and over and over... you get the point. I didn't want to start a job, blink twice, and 10 years had passed and I couldn't find one interesting story about what I accomplished - except maybe saying "I was promoted to XYZ position"

I frequently ask my team what makes them happy and they often say that they like 'working on cool projects' and not doing the same thing over and over. I know I love what I do now as well - so maybe that's a big part of the perfect job... doing something that affords you some creative flexibility to do something 'cool'.

Jackson Miller, another blogger in TN and friend to CentreSource, posted this in his blog... which got me to thinking about the question again: 

> [  
>  I learned two things listening to the podcast today. The first is that I really miss working on software that excites me. The second is that iPods are very cool, and everyone should have one.  
>  ](<http://jaxn.org/blog/archives/730-Podcasting-I-get-it,-or-should-I-say-I-got-one.html>)

I am not a psychologist, but I think the 'cool job' part breaks down into the following (the order of importance is different per person):

1) Continual Learning & Brain Stretching  
** Particularly interesting to technology people

2) Accomplish-able goals  
** This can be completing projects, building a product, or having direct influence on the success of something

3) Likable team members  
** Humans naturally like companionship. The better your work environment, the happier you are on the job. Plus, a strong team can push everyone much further than they could have gone themselves. When properly formed, a team is a powerful force!

4) Adequate Money / Career Growth  
** As a business owner, this one is most interesting to me. I originally felt that money was the most important factor - but two years of 'Risk Analysis' in Finance showed me that its not the #1 item on my list. That's why I said _adequate_ money (you can't be happy while you're stressing about money)

5) Respect  
** This is the most difficult one to gauge. Some people want to be left alone and not bothered when it comes to their work. This translates to 'respect for their work' to them. Others want each of their opinions / suggestions validated and considered - and that equals respect. Either way, the best job can accommodate the 'respect' factor for each team member - and that ultimately lies with the management team.

Some tech teams have such high turnover that one of these areas is seriously lacking. 99% of the time, it probably isn't money.
