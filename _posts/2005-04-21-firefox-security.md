There's a minor backlash against the [Mozilla Firefox](<https://web.archive.org/web/20050422034116/http://www.mozilla.org/products/firefox/>) web browser lately. People are beginning to [scrutinize and criticize its security](<https://web.archive.org/web/20050422043057/http://news.com.com/Firefox+fans+put+new+spin+on+browser+protection/2100-1032_3-5676842.html>), with the implication that perhaps it's not any better than IE.

We will go into the detail on the benefits of using Mozilla Firefox in a later CentreBlog post, but this deserves comment alone. Firefox touts itself as a secure alternative to Microsoft Internet Explorer, and rightly so. But secure does not mean it's bullet-proof. Firefox has security risks by virtue of a) being software and b) still running in a frequently poorly secured operating system like Microsoft Windows. It is and always will be vulnerable to security problems. But, are they comparable to IE's? Hardly. A few reasons why Firefox is still a good idea, security-wise:

  1. Firefox 1.0 was released in November 2004 and is now at 1.03. That's hardly what I'd call an overwhelming series of patches, and further:
  2. These patches were released pro-actively due to community analysis of the code, **not in response to exploits in the wild**. This is a critical difference.
  3. Firefox doesn't use ActiveX. Enough said.



Further, no matter how bad Firefox's security record eventually got, you know what you can always do? **Uninstall it.** Try that with Internet Explorer sometime!

I could go on, but again, we'll be exploring the advantages and disadvantages of using Firefox in a business network environment later, but for now take it from us that Firefox is still a much preferred alternative to IE for a litany of reasons, including security.
