[College freshmen less interested in tech | CNET News.com](<https://web.archive.org/web/20050427005817/http://news.com.com/College+freshmen+less+interested+in+tech/2100-1022_3-5681438.html?tag=nefd.top>)

This is troubling news, and only reinforced by our recent efforts to locate quality talent in the Nashville marketplace. However, I think some deeper analysis may reveal a typical ebb & flow of many industries.

In the fall of 2000, the technology sector appeared to be a haven for anyone that had an idea and technology skills. As the bubble burst, graduates realized that their dreams of becoming dot-com millionaires wouldn't be as easy - and even more realized that they didn't have high paying jobs waiting on them.

The dot com bust and 9/11 put a double-hit on the technology sector. In the same period, many companies were 'testing' the waters in offshore areas such as call centers and software development. Politicians, during the 2004 campaign, heavily played the 'Outsourcing is bad' card and the media embraced the debate.

The combination of these effects would leave a bad taste in any incoming freshman's mind. Does this mean that America is losing its edge? I don't think so.

Most likely, we'll see a rise in demand - leading to lower sector unemployment and higher salaries. Companies will spend more on training their employees and the level 'qualified' workers with experience in various areas will increase. As these high demand pressures build, the media will promote these issues to the population and in a few years, the freshman class will see a spike in those interested in technology jobs.
