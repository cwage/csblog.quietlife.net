[![surface of mars](/wp-content/mars.jpg)](<http://mars.google.com/>)On the day of [Percival Lowell](<https://web.archive.org/web/20060220084811/http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percival_Lowell>)'s birthday, Google has announced the launch of [Google Mars](<http://mars.google.com/>) (think Google Maps but for Mars, get it?). Noel Gorelick (a friend of mine) and Michael Weiss-Malik from Arizona State University worked with Google to combine google maps technology with the data from [THEMIS](<https://web.archive.org/web/20060404180958/http://themis.asu.edu/>), the most detailed imagery of Mars to date.

Full press release below the jump:

> DETAILED PHOTOS OF MARS ARE KEY TO NEW "GOOGLE MARS" WEBSITE
> 
> Tempe, AZ -- A new planet-spanning website, Google Mars (mars.google.com). And a dramatic, simulated flight through the Grandest Canyon in the entire solar system.
> 
> Both new developments have at their heart a gigantic picture-puzzle image of the Red Planet that was created by researchers at the Mars Space Flight Facility of Arizona State University in Tempe, Arizona. 
> 
> This giant mosaic of Mars includes more than 17,000 individual photos meticulously stitched together and blended. The photos were taken by ASU's Thermal Emission Imaging System, or THEMIS, a multi-band space camera. Able to see in 15 visible and infrared "colors," THEMIS was built by ASU scientists and now orbits Mars aboard NASA's Mars Odyssey spacecraft.
> 
> "Mars scientists the world over use THEMIS photos," says ASU planetary geologist Phil Christensen, designer and principal investigator for the THEMIS camera. "It's great that thanks to Google Mars, now everyone, everywhere can explore this neighbor world using their own browser."
> 
> The images in the giant THEMIS mosaic were taken by infrared light and reveal details as small as 750 feet (230 meters) across. They provide a unique view of Mars, melding day and night. Daytime infrared images show landscape features such as craters, dunes, and lava flows. Nightime infrared images show areas where fine particles such as dust predominate instead of rocks and boulders.
> 
> Several areas of Mars with special interest for scientists can be explored in even more detail using the THEMIS mosaic at Google Mars. These include the giant volcano Olympus Mons, the landing sites for the two Mars rovers, Spirit and Opportunity, and the vast equatorial "Grand Canyon of Mars" named Valles Marineris.
> 
> The Valles Marineris mosaic, for example, shows more than twice the detail of the global map. By itself, it sparked an exciting collaboration with digital animators at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory. The result was "Flight Into Mariner Valley," a narrated, 3-minute video that takes viewers on a thrilling aerial tour of the canyon. 
> 
> "Valles Marineris - or Mariner Valley - is the kind of place where scientists want to look at both the small details and the big picture," says Christensen. "It's more than just a spectacular sight - it's also a geological history book of Mars that we've finally begun to open and read."
