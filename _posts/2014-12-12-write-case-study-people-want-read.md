Every good story has a narrative arc. You may remember this from english class...

  1. **Exposition** : The introduction to the story. The characters are introduced, and the setting is revealed.
  2. **Rising Action:** A series of events that complicate matters for the protagonist, creating a rise in the story's suspense or tension.
  3. **Climax:** The point of greatest tension in the story and the turning point in the narrative arc from rising action to falling action.
  4. **Falling Action:** After the climax, the unfolding of events in a story's plot and the release.



![Phone Lady](/content/uploads/2014/12/phone-sales-lady-headset-300x199.jpg)****Any good case study tells a story. So often our case studies read more like a report than a story. Here are five things**not** to do in a case study:

  * **Don't use stats that don't mean anything.**  
As the 19th-century British Prime Minister Benjamin Disraeli said there are "lies, damned lies and statistics." So often you'll see exaggerated numbers thrown out in statistics without any explanation. If possible, focus on the case before and the case after your efforts.
  * **Don't use words that only your team uses.**  
Jargon-filled case studies are all the rage. "At ABC company we used our synergy process to cross-optimize a series of what we called technology-planted roots in order to build a better site." WHAT? Keep it simple. Use common words that people will quickly understand. If you have a strategy process, call it a strategy process... avoid internal jargon.
  * **Images and visual data.**  
Not everything has to be an infographic. If you don't have much data, don't try to make an infographic. It will end up looking like a badly animated cartoon. If you have data, make that visual data easily accessible and clear. If you can summarize an entire paragraph into a single chart, do it! Also regarding images, don't use images you download from Google. For one, often times those images are protected and secondly you will usually end up picking the most obvious image. You know the one...the lady in the black business suit with the headset telephone on!
  * **Build via consensus**  
This is a mistake that I have personally made. Assigning various sections of a case study to team members without a central theme or person in charge is a recipe for disaster. My advice -- assign a single team member to go and interview each person. This will bring "glue" to the story arc.
  * **Write for length**  
Have you ever read a book that basically could have been one chapter yet it was 200+ pages? Don't do that.



So what should you do? Here are five "must-do's" when it comes to case study creation.

  * **Pick the hero**  
Pick the turning point in the story. What single tactic or tool changed the game? By focusing on the hero of the story, you'll ensure your reader gets a clear picture of the value you added.
  * **Dig deeper**  
When interviewing your team to get details on the case study, ask harder questions. I recommend questions like "Why did the client choose us?" or "At what point during the strategy did we uncover the real root issue?"
  * **Design them**  
Don't simply write case studies into Word docs and upload them. Put them in the hands of a competent designer to create layout around the content. This will make your case studies far more digestible and interesting.
  * **Post and share**  
Once you have your case study written, it is no work at all to create an alternate version of that case study. Make sure to consider other outlets where your case study may make sense such as: 
    * Slideshare - post a version of your case study on Slideshare
    * YouTube - record a video talking about your work
    * Webinar - can you turn this case study into advice for others?
    * Be creative and think of other outlets!
  * **Multimedia**  
Mix it up. Use video, audio, downloadable documents and images to convey the story. Don't rely only on the written word. This will appeal to different learning styles.
  * **Follow the narrative arc**  
Make sure to follow the narrative arc. Define the characters and the setting, talk about the struggle, introduce the solution and how you resolved the conflict.
