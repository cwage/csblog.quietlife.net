The sangria went fast at the mixer, with people raving about both kinds we made. We got asked so much last night that we just have to share the recipes with you. Sangria's simple, fun to make, and is a perfect addition to any summer party. Let's go!

**Traditional Red Sangria**[![](/wp-content/uploads/2011/08/4640911585_71e130cb6e-199x300.jpg)](</wp-content/uploads/2011/08/4640911585_71e130cb6e.jpg>)

The key to good sangria is fresh fruit. We used the following, but really anything citrus would be great:

4 Oranges, sliced  
2 Lemons, sliced  
2 Limes, sliced  
1 Diced nectarine  
1 Orange, juiced into mixture

We've also seen pineapple, mango or even grapes go to good effect. Slice all this up and put it in a large bowl.

Then, you'll pour 1 cup of cognac and about a half cup of orange liqueur (we used Triple Sec) into a pitcher. Then add about a half to 3/4 of a cup of granulated sugar to this concoction. Dissolve the sugar into this mix and then pour over the fruit in the bowl and let soak.

[![](/wp-content/uploads/2011/08/Spanish-Demon-Tempranillo-2008-81x300.jpg)](</wp-content/uploads/2011/08/Spanish-Demon-Tempranillo-2008.jpg>)This sangria calls for a dry, Spanish red. We chose the Spanish Demon Rioja Tempranillo, a dry and zesty red with lots of subtle flavor. We used three bottles of this wine, which produces a nice large punchbowl sized amount with a pitcher or so leftover to refill from.

We poured one bottle of red into the soaking fruit concoction and let it sit overnight. The next morning the other two bottles were added and they soaked for about eight full hours.

The final touch: when ready to serve, add about a half liter of seltzer water to the whole batch. It takes the edge off a bit, but also provides some great bubbles for the beverage. And who doesn't like bubbles?!

This sangria was a big hit: fruity, refreshing and a great pair with Mexican food. Enjoy!

**Nectarine Apple Sangria**

This sangria is slightly unusual; in fact, many at last night's mixer had never seen white sangria before! We took a chance on this but it turned out incredibly well. It calls for a dry white wine, which are surprisingly hard to come by in Nashville! Ask your local liquor store for recommendations.

The recipe is pretty similar to the traditional red, but with a few key tweaks:

Fresh fruit, again, is critical. We used:

3 Green Apples, cored and thinly sliced  
4 Fresh Ripe Nectarines, pitted, and thinly sliced  
2 Lemons, thinly sliced  
3 Oranges, thinly sliced  
1 Orange, juiced into mixture

Same as the red sangria, you add 1 cup of brandy, 3/4 cup of sugar, and 1/2 cup of orange liqueur together. Dissolve the sugar, and let soak with the fruit. We added one bottle of our dry white to the mixture and let it soak overnight.

The next morning we added the other two bottles of white and let it sit for eight hours.

Last but not least, adding seltzer when ready to serve gives those nice bubbles and a good mixer flavor overall.

...and that's it! Pretty simple, no? The waiting is the hardest part: this stuff gets extremely fragrant and will emanate longingly from your fridge. Resist the urge and wait the full time, though, as the payoff is so worth it!

As with most recipes, you can add more brandy if you like, more liqueur, more or less seltzer -- everyone has a preference.
