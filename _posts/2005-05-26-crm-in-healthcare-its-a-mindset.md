The following article by Nicholas Holland was printed in the Nashville Medical News in 2005

Today, many Healthcare organizations mistakenly view Customer Relationship Management (CRM) as a software solution. Seeking to improve their 'customer focus,' they purchase a CRM software product and often experience disappointment when they don't see the 'dramatic' results many media outlets frequently report. Gartner Group expert Ed Thompson reports that only 10 to 15 percent of CRM implementations are 'absolute successes,' with up to 15 percent being 'absolute failures.' The success of any CRM application lies solely in the 'customer focused' culture and strategy of the organization.

CRM is an organization-wide strategy to manage customer experience and perception. CRM requires a customer-centric business philosophy and culture to support effective marketing, sales and service processes. Technology solutions can enable effective customer relationship management, provided that an enterprise has the right leadership, strategy, and culture. In short, CRM is not software.

## Developing a CRM Strategy is Critical

The acronym CRM is composed of three powerful terms: Customer, Relationship, and Management. The word 'Customer' represents the intended group your organization serves. 'Relationship,' the most important term, represents the level of trust your organization forms with its intended customers. The final word, 'Management,' represents the effort you put forth to support the 'Customer Relationship.' In its purest form, CRM supports the following areas:

_**Marketing**_

Before any Healthcare organization can conduct business, it must identify target 'customers' for sales (or transaction) opportunities. A CRM strategy for marketing should answer questions such as:

  * Who are my target customers? Where are my target customers located? How do my target customers get information? What methods should I use to reach my target customers? What do my target customers care about?



Once a Healthcare organization can answer these questions from a strategic level, they can select a CRM package to 'manage' their marketing efforts. This will be the first contact with the customer, and thus begins the relationship. Popular CRM applications can provide valuable metrics such as effectiveness of various marketing channels and seasonal trends.

_**Sales**_

As the marketing efforts progress, sales opportunities will arise from the generated leads. An organization should evaluate their CRM strategy to answer questions such as:

  * What is our standard 'sales' process? What do we want to track on sales? How can we differentiate our sales process from our competitors? How often should we 'follow-up' on a potential lead? What metrics do we want to track?



By coordinating an effective CRM strategy with a robust CRM application, Healthcare organizations can effectively track metrics such as the ratio of successful vs. lost sales opportunities, the length of a typical sales cycle, and even the effectiveness of individual salespersons. In addition, differentiators become easier to track such as sending holiday/birthday cards and planning periodic lunches/dinners with key customers.

_**Support**_

Support is a critical part of the relationship, and many customers feel that they are 'forgotten' after the initial sales effort. Surveys show that feeling mistreated is the number one reason customers leave. An organization must evaluate their CRM strategy to ensure it answers questions such as:

  * Should we conduct post-sale surveys? How often should we follow up to maintain the relationship? Do we have a process to handle complaints? Where do a majority of our support calls come from?



By strategically managing the 'support' aspects of the customer relationship, healthcare organizations can effectively use the data in their CRM application to identify and correct problematic trends, maintain high-quality relationships with their customers, and even uncover valuable opportunities for repeat business.

_**Analytics**_  
The final stage of a successful CRM strategy comes in the form of analysis on the metrics collected by your CRM application. Once your team sets the initial strategy, it will take periodic analysis and oversight to ensure that your daily operations are supporting your CRM strategic focus.

## CRM works in Healthcare

Many companies in the healthcare industry are embracing CRM as a great way to better manage their relationships with patients and other healthcare providers. Pharmaceutical and medical device firms are great examples of companies effectively using CRM strategies and applications to more efficiently build relationships with doctors and health facilities. Various 'elective surgical' facilities are utilizing CRM applications to effectively market, sale, and support their patient base.

Approaching CRM as a cultural and strategic initiative better prepares an organization for the implementation of an actual CRM application. While a majority of organizations follow the standard cycles of marketing, sales, and support - those that effectively implement CRM strategies will enjoy much more success with each stage.
