![UX Design](http://blog.centresource.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/ux-icon-298x300.png)It happens at least once a week. I am meeting with a client and hear a litany of reasons why the "execs" won't pay for a "long UX process." Occasionally, I hear of an executive team that values it but simply doesn't have the time or money to invest in a full UX approach.

The result is that they count on their design team to craft a user experience that looks nice and works well enough. The developers often work directly from the Photoshop mockups and "build with blinders" on throughout the process.

The question is this: _**Who owns user experience?** _

If you are like many, the UX of a product is often classified as a "shared" value. This essentially means that everybody is "sort of" in charge of UX. That way, if things go wrong, we will all share **and** not share in the responsibility. So instead of addressing the real reasons we don't have a true approach to UX, we stick to our disciplines and consider UX a little bit throughout.

**Timeline**

One of the biggest motivations behind short-cutting UX is timeline concerns. Organizations often don't get approved funding before their deadline is already in jeopardy. This hurry up, wait, hurry up system certainly puts some restrictions on implementing a proper UX procedure. Here are a few thoughts and recommendations we've seen work to offset this issue.

  1. Include UX documentation in the presentation to the funding board for approval. Don't go in with a bullet point business plan, but instead go in with a prototype or UX plan.
  2. Avoid linear thinking. Often the process is viewed as completely linear meaning it goes from planning to design to development. However, often development can begin prior to the completion of the UX documentation. While this presents its own set of risks it can be a nice compromise in order to hit the timeline.
  3. Sacrifice is often overlooked! So often we want a UX process to be completed but we want to give nothing more than our standard work day to get it done. If you really believe that a UX process can make the application better, step out and get it done. Put something together and demonstrate that you truly believe it can help the application. Whether they use your advice or not isn't the point, the point is that you will have worked through many of the future issues they will likely face in the coming days. You'll soon be the smartest guy in the room :)
  4. UX is iterative. If your leadership views UX as a "front-loaded" step in the process that may be a critical part of your timeline struggle. Helping them understand the testing and ongoing nature of UX may change their mind. This helps them recognize that you are thinking long term and will continue staying involved after launch rather than simply designing and then waiting for the next product release cycle. Leadership likes this sort of thinking!



**Budget**  
UX costs money. I find that most leaders of companies say they value UX. They like to talk about it in board room meetings and talk about how much time went into thinking through the experience the user will enjoy. But behind the curtain, it may be a footnote in the grand scheme of things. They'll even talk about how "product design" is your best marketing but yet they may not make the investment in talent or time to actually map out the user experience. Here are some tips to combat budget:

  1. Build it into the "BA (business analyst)" or design budget. If we all started baking in UX to our process there wouldn't be any pushback since it would be considered the "standard." Some may push back at this point with concern that it will further reinforce a false assumption that UI and UX are one in the same. However, let's face it...adding a whole additional section to the product budget is often an uphill battle. Work in conjunction with other team and combine efforts. You'll get further ahead working cross-departmentally than as a lone martyr of UX.
  2. Spread the budget into future phases. Since UX is an iterative process, it may make sense to present this budget as a monthly percentage of funds. This will help spread out the pain of a big drop in the bucket.



**Patience**  
Lastly, remember that patience and persistence win. Not everyone is fast to change. Not everyone follows the companies you follow. They are often privy to information you are not. Giving leadership the benefit of the doubt that they aren't "dumb" or just stubborn is a winning strategy. There may be concerns or larger issues you aren't aware of. My advice in this area is to be transparent and ask them their thoughts. Here are some good questions that will work as conversation starters:

  1. "I really believe that a UX team or approach would benefit our organization. Do you think it would?"
  2. "What do you view as the major hurdles to actually tackling the UX opportunity?"
  3. "What have you tried in the past and how can I help?"



If you approach the discussion from this standpoint rather than the age old "I'm going to show my boss how smart I am and how they are totally missing the boat" you'll often find an open door and discussion.

So the ultimate question still remains...who owns UX? Owners are sacrificial, passionate and driven. They focus on the big picture and allow other team members to catch up. If you are working on convincing leadership of investing in UX, start by owning the UX process and soon they will be coming to you for advice. Then you'll know they are listening!
