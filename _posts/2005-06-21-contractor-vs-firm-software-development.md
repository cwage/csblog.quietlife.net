When it comes to navigating the uncharted waters of custom software development, companies often find themselves faced with a tough decision: **Choose a development firm** or **Hire contractor(s)**

The choice isn't easy & in many cases, a firm can choose either path to achieve their final goal. As the old saying goes, "There are many ways to skin a cat..." - and your firm must way the pro's and con's of each factor before making the final decision.

## Factors to Consider when Outsourcing Custom Development

_Note: CentreSource is a project firm_

### _Professionalism_

Professionalism indicates how business-like your project will be handled and executed. Factors include mannerisms, dress, attitude, timeliness, and attendance to work.

**Contractor:** The professionalism of a contractor can vary dramatically. Contractors often maintain the right to work on multiple projects, set their own hours, and take sick/vacation leave when they need it. Mannerisms & dress risks can be minimized by thorough pre-screening & through utilization of a staffing firm. Unprofessional conduct often results in replacing the contractor with a new one.

**Project Firm:** An established, successful firm should portray a high degree of professionalism in their daily operations. The firm sets expectations for employees and clients can depend on that consistency. If a single employee goes on vacation or becomes sick, the firm can employ additional resources to support the needs of the customer. If a customer exhibits unprofessional behavior, they can notify the proper channels within the firm for a resolution.

**Winner:** Project Firm

### _Standardized Approach_

Using a standardized approach assumes the creation of development/design documents, executing the project through standard processes, provisioning for each phase of the development, and handling errors with a systemic troubleshooting method.

**Contractor:** For most contract engagements, it's the responsibility of the hiring company to direct the development initiative. If the hiring company doesn't have the knowledge for such direction, they can seek a contractor that is 'self directed' - or simply choose to contract a project manager as well. In these cases, the development approach can be great - or it can be disastrous. In addition, a contractor's approach may be limited to only certain parts of the project and may omit other areas such as deployment.

**Project Firm:** Any established, successful development firm will have a standardized development approach that should include a clear path for planning, development, design, testing, and deployment. Many project firms also have utilities that assist with the development process such as a project management system, bug reporting applications, and other related systems.

**Winner:** Project Firm

### _Additional Skills_

Additional skills relate to any expertise above and beyond actual development. Examples include graphic design, QA Testing, Project Management, additional programming languages, Database Design, and any other skill a single contractor does not have.

**Contractor:** The additional skills factor is difficult to assess with a contractor when you first hire them. Typically, a company chooses a contractor for a specific skill set - the additional skills are simply viewed as a bonus if they exist. For many projects, a company can use a contract for most of what they need. Inevitably, the company often needs something the contract either 1) can't do or 2) isn't very good at. In these cases, they must seek additional help.

**Project Firm:** A project firm is more likely to have additional skills available to complement a development project - but this is not always guaranteed. For project firms, the amount of additional skills is primarily related to the size of the firm. The greater the size of their staff, the larger number of employees & specialization areas they maintain.

**Winner:** Depends - A well rounded contractor team can be as effective as a small project firm. Larger development firms may have an edge.

### _Team Support_

Similar to writers, software developers can get 'coders block' when they encounter a particularly difficult problem. These roadblocks are frustrating and can cause serious delays & problems on a large scale project. Team support refers to the brain-storm resources available to a developer.

**Contractor:** Team support is very low with contractors. A single contractor operates as an island and only has the Internet and books available when encountering trouble. For small contractor engagements, the hiring company may not even provide books - thus leaving only Internet resources for the contractor. Even in larger contractor teams, the 'team' mentality may not exist and the contractors might not be as open to helping each other.

**Project Firm:** Team support is a powerful, but under-represented feature of a project firm. Developers can (if the culture permits) counsel each other on difficult problems - and the overall knowledge base grows with each new solution.

**Winner:** Project Firm

### _Code Repository_

The great thing about software development is that you don't have to re-invent the wheel each time you start a project. Developers have the luxury of using code from their past projects, as well as code written by other developers.

**Contractor:** The very nature of contractor work doesn't support a contractor taking code from their engagements and using them in other projects. Hiring firms usually ask the contractor to relinquish rights to the code they develop, thus rendering the code ineligible for re-use on subsequent contractor jobs.

**Project Firm:** Project firms have the means to store code from previous projects, as well as the incentive to use it. Whether a firm develops for a flat fee or hourly rate, re-using code increases profit margins & assists the firm in better managing time lines. Clients benefit from a project firm's extensive code repository in the form of lower costs, achieved deadlines, and less exposure to errors.

**Winner:** Project Firm

### _Development Environment_

Developing software is the glamorous part of the equation - but that's not the whole formula. Nobody thinks about the actual development & production environments required for the software.

**Contractors:** In contract engagements, the hiring firm is responsible for the development and production environments. Contractors, as part of their assignment, can assemble these environments at the expense of the hiring firm - assuming they have the experience to perform such a task. Beyond the development environment, the hiring firm is responsible for providing the IDE (integrated development environment) software for the contractor to use when developing the software.

**Project Firm:** Established & successful project firms will have a development environment already setup to accommodate software development for the client. In addition to the development environment, they will have their own IDE software, as well as advanced tools used for rapid & efficient software development.

**Winner:** Project Firm

### _Stable, Long-term Support_

One of the hidden costs of custom software is the on-going support. When a firm strikes out into the realm of custom development, they heavily depend on their developers for support & continued maintenance.

**Contractor:** For support, contractors must be further classified to provide an accurate description of their role. Direct-hire contractors are individuals a hiring company found and hired directly - paying the contractor directly for their work. Staffing-firm contractors represent developers procured and placed by a staffing firm for a specified period or project. Direct-hire contractors are much more dependable for long-term support when compared to staffing-firm contractors. Support issues are sporadic, so staffing-firm contractors are too expensive to keep on call 40 hours a week. Regardless, hiring companies run a risk of losing their contractor support if another engagement takes up all the contractor's time/focus.

**Project Firm:** Established and successful development firms offer support contracts with their development contracts. If the software is programmed efficiently, these support contracts are win-win scenarios for the project firm and the hiring company. The project firm makes additional profit margins on the support contract if the software has few errors, and the hiring firm has piece of mind that their expensive software will operate efficiently & any new errors will be handled promptly.

**Winner:** Project Firm

### _Customer Support_

Software development is complicated and wrought with possible pitfalls. Customer Service levels can really make or break a projects success. Bad attitudes, apathy, and chronic mistakes can be detrimental to a custom development project.

**Contractors:** The customer service element is very simple for contractor relationships. The hiring company directly tells the contractor if anything is not going well with the project. The contractor's attitude will ultimately determine the company's feelings about customer service. If the attitude is acceptable, the work will continue. If the hiring company feels it reaches an impasse such as bad attitude, apathy, or chronic mistakes - then it can replace the contractor.

**Project Firm:** For a project firm, customer service really depends on their mission & values. As with so many other businesses, some firms treat it with a high priority... while others barely give notice to it. In the technology sector, many firms lack adequate customer service policies and take a condescending approach towards the client. For those firms that value customer service, the hiring company will have the ability to escalate their concerns to upper management & get a solution.

**Winner:** Tie

### _Diverse Project Experience_

For many custom software projects, there is not precedence to follow - thus the reason it needs to be custom. As such, experience plays a vital role in the development process.

**Contractor:** Experience is really commiserate with the person, so the hiring firm must consider the level of experience needed for their project. Lower experience levels can result in longer development cycles since the developer will be approaching each problem 'fresh'.

**Project Firm:** Similar to the contractor, experience is commiserate with the person placed on the project by the project firm. However, in stark contrast to the contractor, the overall project benefits from the diverse project experience of the entire firm. This is similar to the Team Support factor.

**Winner:** Project Firm

### _Project Management_

In custom development, the outcome of the project can be determined before a single line of code is ever typed. Project Management sets the tone, pace, and efficiency of a project - from the development plan to the actual accountability of execution. Deadlines, deliverables, and detailed communications are vital aspects to a successful project.

**Contractor:** For a large project, the hiring firm can contract excellent project managers. For smaller projects, contracting a full time project manager is too costly. In these scenarios, the hiring firm must provide some form of project management to ensure the success of the development process. The hiring firm is also responsible for providing project management tools ,such as Microsoft Project, for the project.

**Project Firm:** Established and successful project firms will have a project management element for any of their custom development jobs. Project management, for the project firm, manages deadlines, scope creep, and ensures that the client is satisfied with the deliverables provided. For smaller projects, the project firm can use a Project Manager on multiple projects - spreading the cost & benefits among multiple clients.

**Winner:** Depends on the size of the project. For smaller projects, the Project Firm wins. For larger projects, its a tie.

### _Development Cost_

Custom software development is not inexpensive & many firms can quickly find themselves overwhelmed by all the costs associated with their new software. Costs come from various areas such as labor, tools, hardware/software, and procurement of ancillary skills. Labor is always the most expensive cost.

**Contractor:** If a firm finds their contractor directly, the cost can be very low - strictly a factor of negotiating and reaching a compromise. In the case of a staffing firm, the contractor's hourly rate will receive a mark-up of approximately 30-60 percent of their personally requested hourly rate. The staffing firm receives the mark-up and maintains control of the contractor. The hiring firm will also be responsible for procuring the development environment, development tools, resources such as books, and miscellaneous items such as office space & electricity.

**Project Firm:** Pricing varies with project firms, often based on a project scope or a set hourly rate. Rates are much higher than contractor rates and are typically factored on market demand. The project firm provides a turn-key solution for the hiring firm and this is factored in their price.

**Winner:** Contractor
