I saw [this today](<https://optimizely.appspot.com/pricing>) while looking around at possible A/B testing vendors and had a bit of a chuckle. My inner-consumer (we all have one) was "Wow! I can have the CEO's phone number, so I will always get great service!" My inner-cynic was thinking more along the lines of "How useless is that guy or gal going to be when I actually have a problem or question?"

That got me thinking about the larger question of how to take care your customers. In the [Optimizely](<http://www.optimizely.com/>) model, the more the customer pays, the better the support. If you are okay with just using forums or reading through the documentation, go for $19/month. Want to send an e-mail to a support team member? $79/month. Need to talk to a live human being? $399/month. Getting the CEO's phone number can be assumed to be quite a pricey endeavor.

There are two reasons that a company might opt for this tiered model. The first explanation is simply economics, meaning that a customer that pays more is actually funding the level of support they receive (presuming that internet-based support is cheaper than email, which is cheaper than phone). The second one is to steer customers to a higher service tier by showing the value of moving up one more level.

I was talking to a friend of mine about their switch to just providing support via e-mail and online ticket tracking. As a growing business, hiring and training an armada of support specialists to answer all of the phone calls was extremely cost prohibitive, and was actually going to be rather ineffective for anything beyond basic user questions. For web-based companies, this is usually a no-brainer -- the guys with all the answers are usually not the ones answering the phones. For more traditional companies, like a lawnmower manufacturer, this might not be the case.

So what about your business? If you unplugged the phone tomorrow and just offered sales and support over e-mail, what would happen? Would customer satisfaction actually increase, or would people go elsewhere in a panic?
