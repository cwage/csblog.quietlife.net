![Browser Updates](/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/ie-chrome-firefox-300x223.jpg)In the world of web and application development, one thing that seems to creep up more times than I care to count, once launch has happened, is ongoing site issues. There are many reasons for this, but one recurring theme that we see centers around updates to, and new versions of, internet browsers. Without having a crystal ball, there is no sure-fire way for a development team to know exactly what changes will occur when a browser pushes out an update. This typically isn't a concern when a browser jumps from one version to the next, but if your site was built three years ago and three versions have already come and gone, you could be in trouble.

Think of it this way. If you have an iPhone 3G, will it work? Sure, but will it work as well as iPhone 6? Absolutely not. Like most of you, I've had numerous versions of the iPhone and I can tell you from experience that once your phone becomes two or more versions old, things start to act wonky. Maybe the face button stops working properly, or it restarts on it's own every now and again. Either way, when technology changes, glitches happen.

At Centresource, we strongly recommend continuing to audit the code base of your application. This will allow you to better prepare for changes in technology that were unknown, or unheard of, when your product was initially built. The benefit of doing this is to be able to be prepared ahead of time, rather than scrambling to react and fix broken things when an issue rears it's ugly head. The goal is to keep your product as current as possible and not encounter any hiccups along the way. But, beware, browsers _**will**_ update and issues _**will**_ appear. Being as prepared as possible can save you additional funds down the road.
