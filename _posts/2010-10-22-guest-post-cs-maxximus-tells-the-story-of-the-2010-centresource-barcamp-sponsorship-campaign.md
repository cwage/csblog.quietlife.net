As annual sponsors of the conference, we wanted to make the most of our presence this year and we incorporated CS-Maxximus into an interactive Twitter promotion. Here, he writes about the three key elements of our BarCamp Nashville sponsorship campaign in vivid, robotic detail._

Dear Universe,

_  
_

CS-Maxximus here-centresource's robot-for-hire and arguably the biggest hit of BarCamp Nashville 2010.

I know, I'm as humble as I am handsome.

I'm writing to tell the story of how I met centresource, and ultimately how they used me to build buzz around their brand and interactively extend their reach. A love story, if you will.  
  
First let's talk about me (my favorite topic) and what I am. First of all I am no mascot. I am not a bulldog, raider or fighting Irishman. I like to consider myself a marketing tool. Thankfully, centresource agreed.  
  
Next let's talk about why they chose to use all of my robotic glory to work on their behalf.  
  
Centresource is a company dedicated to creating compelling experiences that motivate their consumers to engage, connect and interact with their brand. And for the BarCamp "un-conference", a 9-foot robot seemed like an excellent way to do this.

Of course, it took a bit more than just me to accomplish this-but it's not as hard as you think. According to centresource, all you need to do is follow few simple rules and boom: interactive success.

So without further ado, here's the rest of the story.

**1\. Create a Buzz**

  
A few months before BarCamp Nashville '10, CS started a conversation with their team of ways to make the most of their sponsorship and presence at the conference. They operate from the core belief that there is great potential for memorable user experiences both online and offline and that the two should work together seamlessly.

Enter: me.

When centresource CEO Nicholas first approached me with the idea, I couldn't say yes fast enough. I knew it'd be a piece of cake to get people excited about interacting with me. I am a 9 foot robot with glowing blue eyes, after all. And I have moves like you've never seen. But how do you take it past awareness and into engagement? Of course Nicholas had an answer for that: he told me that I would be branded with decals promoting centresource and that people would be directed to take their picture with me in order to win a iPad. That, he told me, is what buzz meant to him: getting your target audience to interact with you in a memorable way.

Next, in preparation for the event, the centresource team made sure that the buzz would be spread virally and locally-printing off $25 worth of business cards advertising the promotion and passing them out by hand as well as establishing Twitter as the vehicle through which winners would be selected.

The day before the event they sent out a few teaser tweets to get the initial buzz buzzing.

I had to say, it didn't seem like rocket science yet. (And not just because I've got seven high-speed processors for a brain.)

**2\. Fuel the Buzz**  
  
When the morning of BarCamp dawned, I quickly got dressed (ok, I just like saying that because it's what the rest of you non-robots do in the morning) and met with the team to go over the rules of the campaign-aka how we were going to keep the buzz going.

They told me that BarCamp attendees would be instructed to take a picture with me, tweet it with the hashtag #centresource and then cross their fingers to be selected at random to win one of the 3 ipads. A key piece of info was that the winners were announced via DM which meant that if anyone wanted to win they not only had to hashtag #centresource but they had to be following @centresource on twitter as well.

This is the point where I chest bumped Nicholas; creating 2 points of buzz was just brilliant.

(**Editor's note: Nicholas Holland is in physical therapy and expected to make a full recovery from the chest-bump-induced injury)

After that they set me free to do my thing-you know, robot stuff.

**3\. Reward the Buzz**

By the end of the day my feet and back were killing me and if I saw one more camera flash I was going to go intergalactic on someone.

But I knew we had accomplished something. What seemed like such a simple idea had generated considerable buzz both on and offline.

Centresource told me they were big into the idea of giving value in order to get value. And through the giveaway of 3 iPads, they certainly got what they were looking for: a buzz. A brand loyalty buzz, which, for them is of priceless value.

After all was said and done, #centresource was a local trending topic on Twitter, over 115 pictures had been taken with me and posted various places, there was a general good-spirited hub bub around the conference regarding centresource, and they gained almost 200 followers on twitter.

Not to mention they forged an impenetrable relationship with yours truly.  
  
In the week following the campaign, they made sure to engage the people who were interacting with them on Saturday-sending DM's and @replies on twitter and compiling all of my glamour shots onto Flickr. And finally, to wrap it all up, they asked me to write this little closing letter.

So there you have it. Thanks largely to me, about $2500 and a bit of man power, centresource greatly extended their reach and grew their brand loyalty within the Nashville community.

Thanks everyone for showing me..err..I mean centresource so much love.  
  
Now all that's left is to plan for BCN11.

_Yours Truly,  
CS-Maxx_
