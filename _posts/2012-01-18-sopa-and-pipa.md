Today the internet is taking a stand against the Stop Online Piracy Act & the Protect IP Act, commonly referred to as SOPA and PIPA, respectively. You've probably seen these protests take shape in various creative, stark forms. Our favorite - you guessed it - comes from Tumblr, who blacked out actual posts from their users, across every single Tumblr Dashboard. That's millions of words and thousands of posts, hidden from view:

![](http://5.mshcdn.com/wp-content/gallery/sopatimeline/tumblr-censored.png)

Similarly, Wikipedia has boldly pulled their whole site down for the entire day, letting users understand first-hand what's at stake.

Even Google has made a statement about these acts today:

[![](https://csblog.quietlife.net/wp-content/uploads/2012/01/googlesopa.png)](<https://csblog.quietlife.net/wp-content/uploads/2012/01/googlesopa.png> "googlesopa")

The problem with these bills is the seemingly innocuous nature of them. "Stop Online Piracy"? "Protect Intellectual Property"? We are all for curbing piracy, but this bill takes the term "piracy" to a whole new level. Typical piracy acts (like the DMCA) strive to curb illegal downloading and streaming, but SOPA and PIPA are after the actual sites hosting the content.

The basic idea is this: if your site aggregates or host content that can be tied to a copyright, your site can be shut down. That means Twitter, Flickr, Tumblr, Facebook, YouTube, Vimeo and other aggregating sites could be taken out of operation. That also means that the creative use - memes - of copyrighted content online would no longer be considered parody (a First Amendment right) and would be considered an illegal act.

While this does provide additional control to copyright owners, the problem remains the same: the will of the few controls the freedom of many. Major copyright owners include NBCUniversal and Disney; under SOPA and PIPA, these content juggernauts would be essentially given free reign of what content can exist freely on the web, without much regulation. This hints at a dim future of the Web, a preview of which can be found in these online protests today.

Do your part: [write your Congress representatives](<http://americancensorship.org/>) and let them you know oppose this restriction against free speech.
