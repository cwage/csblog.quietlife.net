This Christmas will be a happy one... I finally found a few ecommerce sites that sell a worthy line of Brainteasers, Puzzles, and Secret Boxes.

I am a puzzle enthusiast... For years I've enjoyed a variety of brainteasers and puzzles ranging from textual problems to the physical puzzles you have to figure out with your hands. Basically, I like anything that challenges my logic.

In high school, I received a wooden snake-block puzzle that I absolutely fell in love with. It took me hours to figure it out, but once I did - I had just as much fun actually watching others stress over it and then showing them how fast I could do it. A little egotistical - but the point is that I never got bored of that thing.

The trouble was, I could never find a good selection of comparable puzzles. Cracker Barrel has the old-timey iron puzzles (which are great), so I had a few of those... The Discovery Store in the mall always had a few wooden puzzles that were acceptable... But after a few years, I had seen all there was to see in these retail outlets.

In 2004, I saw a great show on TV about [Japanese Puzzle boxes](<http://www.go-star.com/antiquing/puzzleboxes.htm>) (very ancient) that were fascinating. Intricately carved pieces of wood were assembled to create boxes that required a certain order of 'Moves' to unlock the box. Some of the boxes required over 50 moves!!!

That same show also demonstrated varying Kumiki Puzzles (meaning "to join wood together"). If a single piece was removed, the entire structure lost its integrity and would fall apart.

Needless to say, I was hooked :)

That Xmas season, I searched the web for those boxes & other physical-form logic puzzles. No luck... again, I found a few items here and there - but I had solved most of them. I gave up and assumed I'd have to find them in a catalog or just keep my ear open going forward.

Fast-forward to 2005... As my family asked me for my Xmas list, my memory was sparked for my search for Japanese puzzle boxes. This time, I struck major gold!!!

Google delivered in spades for my search term "wooden puzzle box". Most of the results provided great value to my search. I am not sure why it was so difficult last year, but I am definitely happy with the results.

I am also amazed at how the Internet is maturing and great ecommerce sites are popping up to appeal to eccentric tastes (like puzzle boxes). I think its a great testament to the maturity of the Internet and us as educated users.

Here is a brief description of the great sites I found and some of their fantastic treasures:  


* * *

[Bits and Pieces](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051128022219/http://www.bitsandpieces.com/>) \- This was the first site I visited and I really loved their collection. I've seen a few of their puzzles, but most were new to me. I also loved the diversity of their Brainteasers, Wooden / Metal Puzzles, and Secret Boxes (often called Japanese Puzzle Boxes).  
Favorite Puzzles: [Cube in a Cube](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051129015116/http://www.bitsandpieces.com/products/sku-07-7808__id-67.html>), [8 Band Sheik Ring](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051129015635/http://www.bitsandpieces.com/products/sku-08-7652__id-30.html>), [Secrets of the Pyramid](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051129013244/http://www.bitsandpieces.com/products/sku-07-6066__id-2.html>), and the [Golf Globe](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051129015345/http://www.bitsandpieces.com/products/sku-08-6097__id-31.html>).  
_The Golf Globe is a major time-waster at work, so be warned if you purchase it._

[Cleverwood](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051127092342/http://www.cleverwood.com/>) \- While their website could definitely use a face lift, I can deny breadth and depth of their selections. They serve as a great aggregator of many varied designers/artists of Puzzle Boxes and Kumiki Puzzles.  
Favorite Puzzles: [Japanese Castle](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051129012010/http://www.cleverwood.com/castle.htm>), [Dragon Cube](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051129013749/http://www.cleverwood.com/dragon_cube.htm>), [Heartwood See Saw Box](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051129014059/http://www.cleverwood.com/seesaw_puzzle_box.htm>), and the [12 move - Muku Yosegi](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051129012810/http://www.cleverwood.com/12move2.htm>)

[Puzzle Master](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051127174712/http://www.puzzlemaster.ca/>) \- This site is the most comprehensive site I found. It has puzzles from all over & many that I had never seen before. For me, I really loved the color coded difficulty levels! Another unique feature is the extensive collection of foreign inspired puzzles they have (aren't they all?). I was most impressed by one of their marketing methods - they actually keep the solutions so that you have to request the solution if you're stumped. This achieves two things... One, its great for puzzle lovers that don't want to be tempted by the solution in the box. Two, its great for them because they can see how difficult the puzzles are and get you to sign up on a mailing list if you need the solution!  
Favorite puzzles: [The Egg](<http://www.puzzlemaster.ca/zoom.php?id=323>), [Cast Ring II](<https://web.archive.org/web/20071230191505/http://www.puzzlemaster.ca/zoom.php?id=322>), [Cast News](<https://web.archive.org/web/20071213143420/http://www.puzzlemaster.ca/zoom.php?id=311>) (no idea what it does, but it looks wicked), and the [Catacombs](<https://web.archive.org/web/20020822223555/http://www.puzzlemaster.ca/zoom.php?id=29>)

[Puzzle Box World](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051128051204/http://www.puzzleboxworld.com/>) \- Don't let the poor quality of their website fool you... This website has some of the most beautiful Japanese Puzzle Boxes I've ever seen. With boxes ranging in all sizes (suns is the measurement of Puzzle Boxes) and designs, Puzzle Box world really does boast an impressive collection. I was particularly interested in some of their 72 move boxes - very intricate and very difficult! I don't have a favorite since there are so many beautiful designs here... Don't forget to check out the Moroccan and Costa Rican boxes as well!

[Cubic Dissection](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051129011612/http://www.cubicdissection.com/>) \- This isn't a traditional Puzzle Box site, and that's why I wanted to comment on it! Their puzzles have some distinctly unique characteristics - and I was amazed at all some of the ingenious designs. Check out Robert Yarger's "[Stickman #6 Grandfather Clock](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051129005744/http://www.cubicdissection.com/html/purchase/smno6.html>)" puzzle - its incredible. Also, note Tom Lensch's "[Punch Card](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051129011035/http://www.cubicdissection.com/html/purchase/punch.html>)" puzzle.

[Frik-n-frak](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051129024342/http://www.frik-n-frak.com/>) \- Similar to Bits and Pieces, this site has a great selection of Puzzles and Brainteasers. They have some pretty unique puzzles and I especially liked the plastic puzzles. I look forward to a challenge, but the '[Family Jewel 3D](<https://web.archive.org/web/20060216103038/http://www.frik-n-frak.com/product.asp?itemid=1556&catid=106>)' looks ridiculously difficult given the lack of patterns and glossy shine. I also thought the [Cerebral Rings](<https://web.archive.org/web/20060510005215/http://www.frik-n-frak.com/product.asp?itemid=1551&catid=106>) looked great!

Mr.Puzzle (Australia) - Australia really serves up some great mechanical puzzles with this site! I really like how classy some of the puzzles are. For example, the site has 2 puzzles that go along with a nice [bottle of wine](<http://www.mrpuzzle.com.au/prod394.htm>). Also (and to be fair, I've seen this on other sites), it has a neat Puzzle Alarm clock that requires someone to put it back together before it shuts off! My favorite part of this site is that it sorts the puzzles in levels of difficulty ranging from 1 to 10. This should help new comers & ensure they don't too frustrated in the beginning!  
Favorite Puzzles: [The Ultimate Puzzle](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051130014038/http://www.mrpuzzle.com.au/prod401.htm>) (solving it can win you 1500 Euros!), [Cast Spiral](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051130012900/http://www.mrpuzzle.com.au/prod393.htm>), and the [Trick Opening Lock](<http://www.mrpuzzle.com.au/prod388.htm>)

On a final note, my research uncovered 2 really remarkable puzzles that were so extraordinary I thought I'd share them last. The first is regarded as the [world's most complicated](<https://web.archive.org/web/20050910114724/http://www.johnrausch.com/PuzzleWorld/toc.asp?t=_des/hi001.htm&m=des/hi000.htm>) Puzzle Box. Hiroshi Iwahara's Super Cubi requires 324 moves to reach the secret compartment and easily dwarfs any of the puzzle boxes available today.

My other find is actually somewhat mysterious in that the [Puzzle Museum](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051126221029/http://puzzlemuseum.com/>) doesn't actually know who made it. Regardless, I [found this piece](<https://web.archive.org/web/20051130032604/http://puzzlemuseum.com/month/picm04/200403scan.htm>) to be not only a magnificent puzzle - but also a work of art & craftsmanship.
