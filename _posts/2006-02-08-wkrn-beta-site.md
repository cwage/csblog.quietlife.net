So, WKRN, to much fanfare, announced a preview of their [new website](<http://209.200.24.45/>) today.

I hate to be harsh, because I'm sure they had high hopes, but I have to be honest. It is, by far, one of the worst websites I've ever seen -- and I've seen a lot of bad websites. But being that WKRN has proven to be pretty community friendly, and that we work in the industry, I figure I owe them a little more than "it sucks". Here's a more comprehensive analysis of why this new website is completely awful:

Size
    I'm not sure where to start, so I'll just pick size. As Kevin Newman points out in the [comments here](<http://www.nashvilleistalking.com/archives/2006/02/yall_ready_for_this.html>), the site takes forever and a day to load. Why? The total size of the objects on the page is a whopping **458446 bytes**. That's nearly **half a megabyte**. For a user on dialup, it would take over a minute and a half, assuming optimum speed, to load the WKRN page. I don't need to tell you that if a page takes a minute and a half to load, it might as well not load at all. I don't have any numbers handy, but I assure you that the percentage of people still using dialup in WKRN's viewing area is not one that WKRN should feel comfortable alienating entirely from their site. It's even slow enough that some broadband users would give up on it.
Flash abuse
    Now I am not entirely a flash-hater, but it is not a panacea. Flash can be used to make a really slick site, but you have to be willing to accept that it will alienate a percentage of viewers who a) don't have Flash installed, or b) have problems caused by the Flash. That may be acceptable for a website for, say, a musical artist. But for a TV news station? Courtesy of [Jeffraham Prestonian](<http://jprestonian.blogspot.com/>), here is what the new website looks like to someone without Flash:

I have no idea if it's supposed to look like this, but I hope not. I have seen weird issues with Flash displaying text properly in Linux. Who knows why, but it's another reason why relying on Flash alone for your entire site is a bad idea if accessibility is your goal, which brings me to: 

Usability
    The next test of a good website should be its usability. This site fails. It doesn't even work in my browser. Nothing works. I have no idea where to even start navigating this site, because there are, as far as I can tell, something like 3 or 4 different menus, none of which even work.
Accessibility
    I saved the worst for last. A news site should be accessible. I am sure that when web design seminars get to lectures about accessibility, they probably use news sites as a hypothetical example of a website that should be absolutely accessible to everyone, sparing no cost. A news site should make the availability and usability of their content priority #1. WKRN's site completely flunks this test. Are you blind or deaf? Are you using a modem or even low-bandwidth broadband? Are you using a mobile device? Good luck using WKRN's site. [This site](<http://www.tawdis.net/taw3/cms/en>) will run a URL through a suite of accessibility tests as per the W3's [web accessibility guidelines](<http://www.w3.org/TR/WAI-WEBCONTENT/>). WKRN's new site raises so many warnings it would take weeks to go through them all.

In conclusion, this site looks terrible, is nearly non-functional, takes long enough to load that it alienates any non-broadband users, or any blind/deaf users. It's awful. WKRN could have taken the opportunity in their new website to embrace some technologies that are really furthering the flow of information and accessibility, as they have with [nashvilleistalking.com](<http://www.nashvilleistalking.com/>): RSS, XHTML/CSS compliance, much-vaunted AJAX/"Web 2.0" niftiness. Instead, this site looks like it was designed for 1999, and it's bad even for that time period. I hope that WKRN didn't pay anyone for this site. If so, they deserve to get their money back so they can try again.
