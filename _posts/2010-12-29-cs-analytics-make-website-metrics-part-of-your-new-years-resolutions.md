Some old dude.

[Fredrick Taylor](<http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_Winslow_Taylor>) was a renowned engineer whose pioneering work in scientific management lead to teaching the old adage that one "can only manage what you can measure" that you will find is the _modus operandi_ of most managers you will encounter in business. Particularly applied in motion study, Taylor and later work by Frank Gilbreth revolutionized the way that a laborer went about their job -- finding ways to maximize productivity rather than simply increasing the labor force.

But who really cares about what some guy did back in the early 1900s?

Okay, so I do not really care that much either. But that basic concept of managing through measuring is important when thinking about your website analytics. It is also what guides you to improving its conversion rate.

### Why care about analytics anyway?

Valid question. I know many people who have a website that do not bother to check in periodically on their visitor traffic. And they get by just fine. It could be that they do not know how to install a product like [Google Analytics](<http://www.google.com/analytics>) or Omniture, but more than likely it is simply a question of time. It takes time to pour over the data and formulate a plan of action.

You pay attention to your website analytics because there is a pretty good chance that you did not build the website just for yourself. There has to be a certain amount of curiosity about what your visitors care about when coming to your site, where they came from, and how often they come back. Building the case for measuring your website's performance metrics is as easy as justifying why you built it in the first place.

### How do I get started?

I'll speak to Google Analytics because it is a) free and b) rather easy to use. At the most basic level, to use Google Analytics involves copying and pasting a code into the backend code of your website. Now, there are tons of other things you could do, but that is the most critical step in setting up and configuring the profile. Most content management systems offer a plugin that automate this for you, but if not, it typically is no more complicated than downloading a file from your webserver, updating it, and uploading it again. Google has put together [some instructions for doing this](<http://www.google.com/support/googleanalytics/bin/answer.py?answer=174090>). Obviously, [I work for an interactive agency that would be happy to help](<http://www.centresource.com/contact-us>).

Once you have it installed and can see that data is coming in, you will move on to the next important step: actually paying attention to the data.

### I'm collecting data, now what?

This is where the "time" factor comes in. I recommend checking in on your website performance monthly, but some industries might require a bit more frequency. I usually approach an analytics report by asking a few questions, and then going to find the answers. Here are a few that generally matter.

  * How many (total unique) visitors did I get last month?
  * How does that compare to the previous month? To the previous year?
  * How long did a visitor spend looking at my home page? Another important landing page?
  * Can I see how many people completed a conversion on my site (filled out a form, placed an order, etc)? Can I compare that to the previous month or year?
  * What page saw the greatest increase in traffic since last month?
  * What happened on the day where my traffic spiked? Was it a blog post or another site linking to mine?
  * Do I get more traffic on certain days of the week?



If you could answer five of those questions, you would be well on your way to getting a better handle on your website analytics. Answer all of them plus a few of your own making and you will be doing a lot better than many of your peers.

### Great, but how can I save myself time?

Automated reporting is an easy way to save yourself a bit of time. At minimum, you can get a passing glance at the reports each month so that if questions ever arised you can pull it out of your archived folder. This also sets up a habit -- that monthly reminder prompts you to reflect a bit on what you have been doing to improve the conversion rates.

Take a quick look at this tutorial for [setting up an automated analytics report](<http://www.google.com/support/analytics/bin/answer.py?hl=en&answer=57163>). Go ahead and have them sent to you and perhaps one other person (to keep you accountable for them). It is a lot harder to archive an e-mail when you know someone else got it, too.

### But what if I don't like what I see?

That's the value in analytics. Knowing what disappoints you (low visitor counts, low conversion rates, high bounce rates, etc.) means that you can take tangible steps to address them. That is a lot better than just saying "I don't like the blue on my web site, let's change it something else." These are things you can measure rather than just gut instincts.

There are a few approaches to improving website metrics, but they usually boil down to two tactics: increase exposure (through advertising, publicity, etc.) or increase conversions (through A/B and multivariate testing). The latter is usually the more cost effective approach -- a well-run A/B test can take a conversion rate of five percent and double it to ten percent without increasing overall visitor traffic. By the same token, it would require enough of an advertising to double your visitors to have the same net effect.

Maybe Fredrick Taylor wasn't crazy -- regularly reviewing your analytics and running experiments to increase your conversion rates should be a cornerstone of your interactive strategy.
