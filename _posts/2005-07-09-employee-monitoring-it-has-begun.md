Ever feel like you're being watched? Trust your instincts, it's most likely true. Employee monitoring, while old in concept, finds itself rapidly evolving thanks to the information age. Due to the advancements in technology, firms can affordably monitor, record, and analyze the daily actions of an employee such as Internet usage and even their physical location. As we progress into the next century, employee monitoring will play a powerful role in shaping both performance and social aspects of our business environment.

## What can be monitored?

By far, the most common form of employee monitoring is time & attendance tracking. Most firms have varying degrees of time & attendance systems ranging from simple clock in/out systems to complex programs tracking when users log into their computers & use specific applications. Employees come to expect this form of monitoring and it plays a vital role in the performance of most firms.

The fastest growing area of employee monitoring relates to Internet usage. Companies are rushing to implement systems that track web surfing, instant messaging, emails, and other Internet related technologies. The rapid spread of these systems is twofold: 1) They are inexpensive to implement and 2) Internet related activities account for a large source of 'distractions' in corporate America. These systems are relatively new and are generating intense negative reactions from employees.

Pervasive Employee Monitoring is a relative newcomer to the field. The recent proliferation of GPS (global positioning system) and RFID (radio frequency identification) technologies now provide companies with an inexpensive way to track the physical presence of employees. NexTel recently announced mobile phones with GPS features that allow employers to instantly track the physical location of their employees. Even more advanced, Omron has developed an RFID system that helps manufacturing firms track an employee's exact movement throughout the day. Physical presence tracking is highly controversial and much debate will ensue over the upcoming years.

## Reasons for Monitoring

As businesses seek to optimize all aspects of their operations, employee monitoring becomes an attractive way to identify bottlenecks, inefficiencies, and operational risk factors. In the past, employee monitoring was inefficient, resource intensive, and difficult to justify for many businesses. Today, technology has dramatically reduced the cost to monitor employees - and analysis of the data is instantaneous. As the cost barrier is lowered, firms can justify the benefits of monitoring their employees.

One of the most compelling reasons companies institute employee monitoring lies in the ever increasing risks associated with the internet. A survey from Vault.com states that 37.1% of people said they surf the web constantly at work, with another 31.9% saying they surfed a few times per day. In 2002, Nielsen NetRatings found that over 44% of corporate employees actively used streaming media during the day. Shockingly, ComScore Networks found that 37% of US workers had visited an X-Rated website while working.

These compelling statistics show a relatively new phenomenon of distractions related to the Internet. For an employee that makes $21/hour, 20 minutes a day of inappropriate activities results in approximately $35/week of value lost if the employee. In a year, that amounts to $1750+ of lost productivity per employee! The costs are actually higher when related to web surfing since many employees become infected with Spyware (higher IT support costs) and use valuable bandwidth for their streaming media.

## Controversial Issues

Employee monitoring can be very controversial and firms should be careful how they execute such programs. Overly aggressive monitoring programs can be detrimental to employee morale and create headaches for the firm when it discovers 'gray' areas. For example, many businesses have trouble handling issues as simple as 'Internet Usage' violations such as an employee checking their brokerage accounts - often filled with stock from their own company.

Firms must also prepare for a dual mentality from management. Upper management approves of employee monitoring & related restrictions for regular workers, but often wants the monitoring and restrictions removed from them. How will a firm handle a usage violation by the CEO such as checking sports scores on ESPN? These tough issues create additional layers of complexity to an organization and can instantly develop an environment of fear and bitterness.

## Conclusion

Employee monitoring will continue to increase as the systems used to support them become less expensive and easier to maintain. Businesses constantly seek to maximize operational efficiencies and employee monitoring is a powerful tool to support that goal. As technologies advance, the largest barrier to successful employee monitoring will be the controversial issues related to privacy and employee morale.

### Monitoring Scenario

Through relatively recent advancements in technology, employers are capable of monitoring virtually every part of an employees work day - even they're physical presence! To better illustrate what's available on the market to monitor employees, I've provided a scenario for illustrative purposes.

Tom pulls into her parking spot at 7:45am. She exits the car, walks into her works lobby and scans her ID badge on the card reader to enter the main manufacturing area. After putting her lunch in the fridge, Tom goes to the computer terminal to clock in & picks up her tracking badge before proceeding to her post on the assembly line.

**Monitored items:**

  * Video surveillance recorded Tom's entrance to work - from arrival to walking into the lobby.
  * The corporate security system records Tom's card access to the main manufacturing area.
  * Tom's clock-in records her attendance & work-start time in the company's time and attendance system.
  * Relatively new, Tom's tracking badge uses RFID technology to constantly record her position in the manufacturing facility.



Jane grabs a cup of coffee before settling behind her desk for work. She logs into her computer and starts to check email. Jane reads an email from a customer asking if they can get a large order fulfilled within the next month. She opens up the internal website that shows the manufacturing forecasts for the month and sees that they can probably meet the customer's needs. Jane types up a short email stating they can fulfill the order. Once she sends it, she opens up her instant messaging window and chats with Bob in the shipping department about the upcoming deal.

**Monitored items:**

  * Jane's computer login information goes to multiple locations. The employer has the login info go to the security system for tracking, but also to the time & attendance system.
  * The corporate email system keeps a copy of all in/out emails for future retrieval & analysis. Sophisticated analysis software interprets Jane's out-going email as a 'potential agreement' and flags it for review by upper management.
  * Jane's visit to the internal manufacturing statistics website is tracked and recorded by the company's web usage software.
  * The corporate instant messaging system records the chat conversation for both Jane and Bob.
  * As a precaution, the company also utilizes computer usage monitoring software that tracks keystrokes, mouse movements, and time lapsed screen shots.



After the chat with Jane, Bob opens a web page that instantly shows the location of all the company trucks across the nation. He notices that no trucks are available for Jane's potential deal, so he calls a contractor trucking firm to make arrangements. Bob asks the contractor to send the driver over to pick up a GPS enabled mobile phone for shipment tracking.

**Monitored Items:**

  * All phone conversations in the firm are digitally recorded and can be analyzed with voice-analysis software if needed.
  * The GPS mobile phone allows Bob's department to track the shipment being sent by the contract firm.
